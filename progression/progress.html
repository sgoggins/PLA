Background

The flow of students through courses contained within major is of interest to both students and educators. Students may wish to know common course sequences taken by their predecessors: are there many ways to complete the major, or are there paths that lead to better outcomes? Educators meanwhile may seek to understand the level of preparation students have reached as they progress to courses later in the curriculum: are students using introductory course locally, or transferring them in? Are they skipping recommended courses?

Analysis

Sankey diagrams and sunburst diagrams both give general overviews of this flow. Below is a screenshot of a sunburst diagram that shows the flow of physics majors through the University of Michigan's Physics Department (to preserve anonomity we only show screenshots, and an interactive sunburst diagram with synthetic data follows the description of this animation):

This GIF demostrates how, as a user drags the cursor from inner rings to outer rings, arcs light up and "breadcrumb trail" is recorded in the right margin (download images the individual terms: 1, 2, 3, or 4) The inner most ring gives a break down of Physics courses taken by first term Physics majors. It reveals that 52.9% of physics majors take NO physics classes (none) their first term. A fraction of these students go on to take Physics 140/141 their second term (8.75% of all physics majors). A fraction of these students continue to Physics 240/241 in their third term (5.39% of the total), and most of these student continue to 340/341 the very next term (4.38% of all physics majors). Thus we can say that 4.38% of our majors take no physics their first term and then complete the introductory sequence the next three consecutive terms.

Using PLA-MOOC student record and student course tables, our custom R code, and modifications to D3 sunburst scripts (see the source for the page), we can create a fully interactive sunburst diagram where we explain the layout in more detail. Additional work in progress involves the use of Sankey Diagrams to study the flow of students through majors; for our uses, it seems that these will require a more substantial rewrite or reorganization of existing libraries.
